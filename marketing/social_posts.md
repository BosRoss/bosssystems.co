## 1. r/smallbusiness

**Title:** We mystery shopped 50 small businesses during business hours. 62% didn't answer the phone.

**Body:**

I run a small AI automation company and we just finished a research project that kind of blew my mind. We mystery shopped 50 local service businesses — called them during posted business hours, pretending to be a real customer with a real job.

The results:

- 62% of calls went unanswered during business hours
- Average phone experience grade: C-
- Only 8% scored an A or above
- The most common failure wasn't rudeness — it was just nobody picking up

Some other things we noticed:

- Businesses that did answer often had no system for capturing the caller's info. If the person they needed wasn't available, the lead just evaporated.
- Almost nobody asked qualifying questions. They'd answer, give a price, and hang up. No name, no callback number, no next step.
- Voicemail setups were rough. Generic carrier greetings, full mailboxes, or messages that clearly hadn't been updated in years. And the data backs up why that matters — 85% of callers who hit voicemail never call back.

The businesses that scored well had a few things in common: a real human answered within 3 rings, they asked for the caller's name and number early, they had a clear next step (scheduling, quote, callback window), and they sounded like they actually wanted the work.

None of that is expensive to fix. It's just not something most owners think about because they never hear their own phone experience from the customer's side.

If you're curious where your own business would fall, I built a free estimator that gives you a rough grade based on a few questions — no phone call required, takes about 30 seconds: bosssystems.co/grade-free

Happy to answer questions about the methodology or what separated the A-grade businesses from the rest.

I'm a 17-year-old developer from Tyler, TX. I've been building AI phone systems for service businesses (HVAC, plumbing, etc.) and kept running into the same problem: owners have no idea what customers experience when they call.

So I built Phone Grade. For $29, an AI agent calls a business as a realistic customer with a scenario that matches their trade. It evaluates the interaction across multiple dimensions — answer time, greeting quality, information capture, objection handling, next-step clarity — and generates an A-F grade with a detailed report.

Technical overview:

The calling layer uses Retell AI for the voice agent. Each call gets a dynamically generated persona and scenario based on the business type and location. The agent follows a branching conversation tree — it can handle being put on hold, transferred, asked to call back, or sent to voicemail, and each path gets scored differently.

The grading engine runs post-call on the full transcript. It evaluates against a weighted rubric I built from mystery shopping 50 businesses manually to calibrate. Categories include responsiveness (did someone answer, how quickly), information capture (did they get name, number, address), service knowledge (could they speak to what they offer), and conversion mechanics (was there a clear next step).

The report generation is templated but the analysis text is LLM-generated from the transcript, so each report reads as a specific assessment of that call rather than a generic checklist.

Results from our 50-business calibration set: 62% of calls went unanswered during business hours. Average grade was C-. Only 8% scored A or above.

The cost structure is simple: voice API costs are under $2 per call, report generation is negligible, payment is through Square. No subscription model — service business owners are deeply resistant to recurring charges from vendors they haven't built trust with yet.

There's also a free estimator (bosssystems.co/grade-free) that gives a rough self-assessment grade based on a few questions. It serves as top-of-funnel and also helped me validate that the grading rubric maps to owner intuition.

The product: bosssystems.co/grade
