Legal Stuff

Permits and Licenses

Before you take your first paid job, you need a few things set up to stay legal. This is not complicated, but it is important. Here is exactly what you need based on your business type.

Every BPP Business

What Everyone Needs

  • Business License -- Most cities and counties require one. Go to your city hall or county clerk's office and ask for a "general business license" or "business registration." $25-$100/year typical
  • General Liability Insurance -- Protects you if you damage a customer's property or someone gets hurt. You can get this online in about 15 minutes. Try Next Insurance or Thimble. $30-$60/month typical
  • Vehicle -- You need a reliable way to get to jobs. A truck or SUV with space for equipment is ideal. A car works for some businesses (lawn care with a trailer, pressure washing with a small rig).
  • EIN (Employer Identification Number) -- Free from the IRS. Takes 5 minutes online at irs.gov/ein. You need this for taxes. Think of it as a Social Security number for your business.
  • Separate Bank Account -- Open a free business checking account at your local bank or credit union. Keep business money separate from personal money. This makes taxes way easier.
Junk Removal

Junk Removal Permits

  • Solid Waste Hauling Permit -- Some cities require this if you are hauling waste commercially. Check with your city or county waste management department. $50-$200/year
  • Dump Access -- Find your local transfer station or landfill. Most charge by weight ($30-$60/ton). Some require you to register as a commercial hauler. Call them before your first load so you know the process.
  • DOT Number -- Only if you use a truck over 10,001 lbs (like a big box truck). Most people starting out use a pickup truck and a trailer, so this probably does not apply to you yet. Check with your state DOT to be sure.
  • What you do NOT need -- You do not need a contractor's license for junk removal. You are hauling, not building.
Lawn Care

Lawn Care Permits

  • Pesticide Applicator License -- Only if you spray chemicals (weed killer, fertilizer, pest control). If you only mow, edge, and trim, you do not need this. If you want to offer weed treatment later, apply through your state's agriculture department. $50-$150 + exam
  • Trailer Registration -- If you use an open trailer for your mower, make sure it is registered with your state DMV. $25-$75/year
  • Noise Ordinances -- Most cities have rules about when you can run loud equipment. Typical rule: no mowing before 7am or after 9pm. Check your city's noise ordinance.
  • What you do NOT need -- No contractor's license for mowing lawns. No special certifications to start.
Pressure Washing

Pressure Washing Permits

  • Wastewater Discharge Permit -- Some cities require this because dirty runoff water goes into storm drains. Call your city's public works or environmental department and ask. Some cities do not care, others are strict about it. $0-$100/year
  • Chemical Handling -- If you use sodium hypochlorite (bleach) for soft washing, some states want you to have basic chemical safety training. Check your state EPA or environmental agency.
  • Water Access -- You will use the customer's water unless you bring a tank. Make sure to discuss water usage with customers upfront so there are no surprises on their water bill.
  • What you do NOT need -- No contractor's license for exterior cleaning. No plumbing license.

How to Find Your Specific Requirements

Every state and city is a little different. Here is how to find out exactly what YOUR area requires:

  • Step 1: Search "[your city] business license application" on Google. Most cities have an online form or a PDF you can download.
  • Step 2: Call your city hall. Say: "I am starting a [junk removal / lawn care / pressure washing] business. What permits or licenses do I need?" They deal with this question every day.
  • Step 3: Check your state's Secretary of State website for any state-level registrations.
  • Step 4: Search SBA.gov permits guide for your state's specific requirements.

How to Price Your Services

Pricing is one of the scariest parts when you are starting out. Here is a simple formula that works:

  1. Find out what others charge. Search Google for "[your service] prices in [your city]." Call 2-3 competitors and ask for a quote on a standard job. Now you know the range.
  2. Start in the middle. Do not be the cheapest (people think cheap means bad). Do not be the most expensive (you do not have reviews yet). Price right in the middle.
  3. Factor in your costs. Gas, dump fees, equipment wear, and the 20% BOSS split. Make sure your price covers everything and still leaves you profit.
  4. Typical ranges:
    • Junk removal: $150-$500 per load (depends on how much stuff)
    • Lawn care: $35-$75 per yard (depends on size)
    • Pressure washing: $150-$400 per house, $80-$200 per driveway
  5. Raise your prices after 10 jobs. Once you have a few reviews, you have earned the right to charge more. Most people raise prices 10-20% after their first month.

How to Get Your First Customer

Your first customer is the hardest one to get. After that, it gets easier. Here is what actually works:

  1. Start with people you know. Text everyone you know: friends, family, neighbors, parents' friends, church members. Say: "I just started a [business type] business. Do you know anyone who needs [service]?" You are not selling them. You are asking for referrals.
  2. Do your first job free or cheap. Offer one free or discounted job to someone you know. Take before/after photos. Ask them to leave you a Google review. That one review is worth more than the money.
  3. Post on Facebook Marketplace and Nextdoor. These are free. Post with photos, your price range, and your phone number. People in your area are already looking.
  4. Door hangers and flyers. Print 50 simple flyers (Canva is free) and put them on doors in neighborhoods where you want to work. Include your phone number (the BOSS AI will answer).
  5. Tell every person you meet. "I run a [business type] business" should be part of how you introduce yourself. You will be surprised how many people say "actually, I need that."

What to Do When Things Go Wrong

Things will go wrong. That is normal. Here is how to handle the most common problems:

  1. Customer is unhappy with the work: Go back and fix it. Do not argue. Do not get defensive. Say "I want to make this right" and then actually make it right. A customer who had a problem fixed is more loyal than one who never had a problem.
  2. You broke something on the job: Tell the customer immediately. Do not hide it. Offer to pay for the repair or file an insurance claim (this is why you have insurance). Honesty builds trust.
  3. Customer will not pay: Get payment before you leave the job site when possible. If they owe you, send a polite text with the amount and a Venmo/CashApp link. If they ghost you, move on. One bad customer is not worth your energy.
  4. Bad weather or equipment breaks: Communicate early. Text the customer: "Rain tomorrow, moving your job to Thursday. Does that work?" Customers respect communication more than perfection.
  5. You do not know how to do something: YouTube has a tutorial for literally everything. Search "[thing you need to do] tutorial" before the job. And call BOSS if you are stuck.

Frequently Asked Questions

The 10 questions every new partner asks.

How much money can I actually make?+
It depends on how much you work. A partner doing junk removal full-time (5 days a week, 2-3 jobs a day) can make $450-$750/week after costs and the BOSS split. Part-time (weekends only), expect $150-$300/week. These are real numbers based on actual job pricing, not hype. Results vary.
Do I need my own truck or equipment?+
For most businesses, yes. Junk removal needs a truck or a trailer. Lawn care needs a mower, trimmer, and blower (you can start with used equipment for $300-$500). Pressure washing needs a pressure washer ($200-$400 for a good starter). You do NOT need brand new professional equipment to start. Upgrade as you grow.
What does BOSS actually do for me?+
BOSS builds and runs your entire tech stack: an AI phone system that answers your calls 24/7, texts customers back automatically, schedules jobs, follows up for reviews, and handles all the behind-the-scenes stuff you would normally need to hire someone for. You focus on the work. We handle the phones and the tech.
Can I do this if I am under 18?+
Yes, but you need a parent or guardian to sign off. Some states require a parent to co-sign your business license if you are a minor. We will walk you through it. Many successful businesses were started by teenagers.
What if I want to switch business types later?+
You can. Just let us know and we will rebuild your AI system for the new business type. Some partners run multiple services (like lawn care + pressure washing). The phone system handles all of it.
How do I handle taxes?+
As a self-employed person, you will owe income tax and self-employment tax on your profits. Set aside 25-30% of what you earn for taxes. Get an EIN from irs.gov (free, takes 5 minutes). Keep track of every expense (gas, dump fees, equipment, insurance) because those reduce what you owe. We strongly recommend talking to a tax professional before tax season.
What if I am not getting enough customers?+
This is the most common problem. Check the "How to Get Your First Customer" guide above. The biggest mistake is waiting for customers to come to you. You have to go find them. Post on Facebook Marketplace, put out door hangers, tell everyone you know. The AI phone system only works if people know your number.
Can I cancel anytime?+
Yes. There is no contract and no cancellation fee. If you want to stop, just tell us. You keep everything you have earned, and we turn off the AI systems. No hard feelings.
What area can I work in?+
Anywhere in your city and surrounding areas. When we set up your AI phone system, we configure it for your specific service area. You decide how far you want to drive. Most partners start within a 15-20 mile radius and expand as they get busier.
What happens if I have a slow week?+
Slow weeks happen, especially when you are starting. You only pay the 20% split when you actually earn money. No jobs = nothing owed. Use slow weeks to market: post on social media, put out flyers, and follow up with past customers. Seasonality is real (lawn care slows in winter, junk removal picks up in spring). Plan for it.
