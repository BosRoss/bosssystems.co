Learn The Work First

You don't need experience — but practice once before you show up at a customer's house.

What this job is: You show up with a truck, load everything the customer wants gone, drive it to the dump or donation center, and leave the space clean.
  1. 1Visit your local dump before your first job. Search "transfer station near me" or call your city's sanitation department. Drive there, ask what they accept and the cost per load (usually $15–40). Know the hours. This is information you'll need on every single job.
  2. 2Know the donate vs. dump split. Items in good condition — furniture, appliances, tools, clothes — go to Goodwill, Habitat for Humanity ReStore, or Salvation Army. These are free to drop off, which saves you dump fees and boosts your margin. Call ahead to confirm they're accepting.
  3. 3Learn to load tight. Big heavy items (couches, appliances, mattresses) go in first against the cab. Boxes and bags fill the gaps. A packed load is more profitable than a half-empty one. Practice loading and unloading at home — move furniture around until it feels natural.
  4. 4For heavy appliances, rent a hand truck from Home Depot (~$10/day) or buy one for $30. Bend your knees, keep your back straight, use your legs. Never muscle a fridge alone — find someone to help or it waits for another job.
Before your first paying client: Load some heavy items from your own home — furniture, boxes, whatever's around — into a truck or trailer. Make one actual trip to your local dump so you know exactly how it works. When your first job comes in, you'll already know where you're going.

Learn The Work First

You don't need experience — but one practice session on your own driveway is all it takes.

What this job is: You connect a gas pressure washer to an outdoor spigot, run the wand over dirty surfaces at the right angle and distance, and the pressure does the work.
  1. 1Learn your nozzles. They're color-coded: white (40°) for delicate surfaces and rinsing, green (25°) for concrete and driveways, yellow (15°) for tough stains. Never use the red (0°) tip on any surface — it will gouge concrete, shred wood, and put a hole in siding.
  2. 2Distance matters. Hold the wand 8–12 inches from concrete for a normal clean. Too close strips paint and chips soft concrete. Too far does nothing. Work at a consistent 45-degree angle, not straight down — straight down splashes dirty water everywhere.
  3. 3Know what NOT to wash with high pressure: window screens (they blow out), painted wood (strips it), AC units (bends the fins), roof shingles (destroys them), old caulking around windows and doors. Stick to concrete, brick, and vinyl siding on lower settings.
  4. 4Technique: work top to bottom so dirty water runs down over clean surfaces. Overlap each pass by 50%. If using a detergent, apply it first, let it sit 2–3 minutes, then rinse before it dries.
Before your first paying client: Rent a pressure washer and do your own driveway or a family member's for free. Spend 30 minutes just running the wand and getting comfortable with the distance and angles. Take a before/after photo. If you like what you see, you're ready.

You don't need experience — mow your own lawn once with intention and you'll know everything you need.

What this job is: You mow (cut the grass), edge (trim the line where grass meets concrete), and blow (clear clippings off driveways and sidewalks). Most yards take 30–60 minutes.
  1. 1Mowing height. Set the deck to 3–3.5 inches for most residential lawns in summer. Never cut more than one-third of the blade at once — if grass is tall, do a first pass at a high setting, then a second at normal height. Cutting too short in summer heat turns grass brown and gets you a bad review.
  2. 2Order of operations: edge first, mow second, blow last. Edge along the sidewalk and driveway before you mow so the mower picks up the edged clippings. Blow clippings off all concrete surfaces when you're done — nothing should be left on the driveway or porch.
  3. 3Mowing pattern. Drive in parallel lines, slightly overlapping each pass so you don't miss strips. Alternate the direction each visit — if you mow north-south one week, go east-west the next. Grass leans the direction you always mow; alternating keeps it upright and even.
  4. 4What causes problems: mowing wet grass (clogs the deck, leaves clumps), dull blades (tears grass instead of cutting, looks ragged), scalping on hills (lower spots get cut too short — raise the deck slightly). Keep blades sharp — a sharpening kit costs $10 at any hardware store.
Before your first paying client: Mow, edge, and blow your own lawn or a family member's — full sequence, start to finish. Time yourself. A standard yard should take you under 45 minutes once you know what you're doing.

Learn The Work First

This is the most technical service on the list. Watch the videos below, then practice on a real pool before charging anyone.

What this job is: You skim leaves off the surface, brush the walls and floor, vacuum the bottom, and test and adjust the water chemistry with chemicals. Weekly service means the same pool every 7 days.
  1. 1Watch these two videos first — search YouTube for "pool chemistry for beginners" and "how to vacuum a pool manually." Combined, about 30 minutes. The chemistry part matters: bad chemistry = green water, cloudy water, or skin irritation for swimmers.
  2. 2Test strips tell you what to fix. Dip in the water, hold flat for 15 seconds, compare colors. You're checking: chlorine (keep at 1.0–3.0 ppm), pH (keep at 7.4–7.6), and alkalinity (80–120 ppm). Numbers outside those ranges need adjustment before you leave.
  3. 3How to adjust: pH too high → add pH Down (muriatic acid, poured slowly around the perimeter edge). pH too low → add pH Up (soda ash). Chlorine low → add chlorine tablets to the floater or liquid chlorine to the water. Add one chemical at a time, run the pump for 15 minutes, then retest. Never mix chemicals together — add them to the water separately.
  4. 4Cleaning order: skim floating debris first, brush walls and floor second (pushes debris toward the drain), vacuum last. Skimming before vacuuming keeps the vacuum from clogging.
Before your first paying client: Ask a neighbor or family friend with a pool if you can clean it for free in exchange for using it as a training run. Do the full process — skim, brush, vacuum, test, adjust. If the water looks better when you leave, you're ready.

You don't need experience — interior detailing is a skill anyone can learn in one afternoon.

What this job is: You go to the customer's location and clean the inside of their car — vacuum every surface, wipe down the dash, doors, and console, clean the glass, and condition the leather or plastic. You bring all the supplies. Takes 1.5–2 hours per car.
  1. 1Learn what product goes where. All-purpose cleaner for hard surfaces and carpet. Glass cleaner for windows only. Leather/plastic conditioner for the dash and seats. Don't mix them — the wrong product on the wrong surface leaves streaks or damage. Read the label on every product once before you start.
  2. 2Master the sequence. Always work top-to-bottom, inside-to-outside: headliner and visors first, then dash, then doors, then seats, then floors, then glass last. If you vacuum the floors first and then wipe the dash, crumbs fall back down. Order matters.
  3. 3Learn to handle pet hair and stains. Search YouTube: "how to remove pet hair from car seats" and "how to get stains out of car carpet." A rubber glove dragged across the seat pulls pet hair into a pile. Carpet stains need a scrub brush + all-purpose cleaner, let sit 30 seconds, then blot (never rub — rubbing spreads it).
  4. 4Time yourself on your first car. Most beginners take 3 hours because they move slow. Your goal is 1.5 hours for a standard car at full rate. Track your time and you'll naturally get faster after 2–3 cars.
Before your first paying client: Detail a family member's or friend's car for free. Focus on going through the full sequence in order. Take a before photo of the dirtiest spot before you start and an after photo when done — that photo is your most valuable marketing asset.

You don't need experience — cleaning a house is something you can learn to do professionally in one day.

What this job is: You clean someone's home on a schedule — bathrooms, kitchen, all surfaces, floors. You bring your own supplies. Most jobs take 2–3 hours. Clients rebook every week or two, so this business builds recurring income fast.
  1. 1Learn which product goes on which surface. Bathroom cleaner for toilets, sinks, and tubs. All-purpose for counters, appliances, and hard floors. Glass cleaner for mirrors and windows only. Never use the same cloth on the bathroom and the kitchen — keep them separate or use color-coded microfibers.
  2. 2Learn the rule: top-to-bottom, dry before wet. Dust first, then spray and wipe, then mop or vacuum floors last. If you mop first and then wipe the counters, crumbs and drips land on the wet floor. This one rule prevents redoing work.
  3. 3Watch one bathroom cleaning video. Search YouTube: "how to clean a bathroom professionally fast." The bathroom is the room clients judge most — if it smells clean and the toilet is spotless, they think the whole house is perfect. It's 15 minutes of a job if you know the sequence.
  4. 4Build your checklist. Write down every task for every room. Bring it on every job until you have it memorized. A checklist means you never miss the inside of a microwave or the top of the fridge — those are the things clients notice when you skip them.
Before your first paying client: Deep clean your own home using the exact sequence and checklist you'll use for clients. Time yourself. If something takes longer than expected, that's where you need more practice before you're on a client's clock.

You don't need experience — balloon garlands and backdrops are learnable in a single afternoon with a YouTube video and your starter kit.

What this job is: You show up to a party venue an hour before guests arrive, set up balloon garlands, a backdrop, and table decorations, then leave. All supplies come with you. The setup takes 45–90 minutes depending on size. You're done and paid before the party even starts.
  1. 1Learn balloon garlands first. Search YouTube: "balloon garland tutorial for beginners." The balloon decorating strip is a plastic strip with holes — you push balloon necks into the holes to build the garland.   2. 2Learn how to use the hand pump efficiently. Inflate big balloons 80% full, medium 70%, small 60% — overinflated balloons pop when pushed into the strip. Knot each balloon before inserting it.   3. 3Learn backdrop setup. The frame has 4–5 connectable poles. It stands free or leans against a wall. Practice assembling and disassembling it twice so you're not struggling with it at the venue. Fabric drapes over the top bar — clip it evenly so it doesn't sag to one side.
  4. 4Learn what clients actually care about. They want photos. Your setup exists to be in photos. Put the backdrop centered, garland at a height that frames people standing in front of it, and keep the table level and symmetrical. Step back and look at it like a photographer would before calling it done.

You don't need experience — but you do need to know how to handle a dog you've never met before showing up to walk it.

What this job is: You walk dogs on a daily or weekly schedule and watch pets overnight when owners travel. The walking pays per visit; the overnight sitting pays per night. Both are recurring — once a client books you, they rebook every week without you having to ask.
  1. 1Learn to read a dog's body language. A relaxed dog has a loose body and wagging tail. An anxious dog has a tucked tail, flat ears, and looks away. An aggressive dog has a stiff body, raised hackles, and holds eye contact. If a dog is stiff when you first arrive, don't reach for it — crouch down, look away, and let it come to you. Never rush a dog you haven't met.
  2. 2Learn how to handle a puller. Most dogs pull on the leash because they've never been trained not to. Stop walking every time the leash goes tight. Stand still. The second the tension releases, move forward. It takes 10–15 stops on the first walk, then the dog learns you're not moving until they ease up. Don't yank the leash back — it makes them pull harder.
  3. 3Know the basics of every client's pet before you start. Before the first walk, ask: Does your dog get along with other dogs? Any foods they can't have? Vet's name and number? Any medical issues I should know about? Write it down and keep it in your phone. If something happens, you need this information immediately.
  4. 4Always do a meet-and-greet first. Never walk a dog for a client you've never met. Schedule a 10-minute visit with the owner present — let the dog sniff you, practice putting on the leash, do a short walk around the block. This protects you, builds client trust, and lets you find out if the dog has any quirks before you're alone with it.
Before your first paying client: Walk a neighbor's or friend's dog twice for free — including the full meet-and-greet process. You don't need experience — home organizing is a system anyone can learn, and clients pay well because most people never figure it out themselves.

What this job is: You go into a messy space — usually a pantry, closet, or garage — sort through everything with the client, remove what doesn't belong, and create a clean organized system. You charge by the hour. Most jobs are 2–4 hours and clients want you back for every room.
  1. 1Learn the three-pile method. Every item in the space goes into one of three piles: Keep, Donate, or Trash. You guide the client through this — you hold things up, they decide. Don't let them hold the item; when people hold something they always keep it. Your job is to move fast through decisions. "Have you used this in a year?" is usually enough. If they hesitate, it goes in donate.
  2. 2Learn how to group what stays. After the three piles, everything in Keep gets grouped by category and frequency. In a pantry: canned goods together, snacks together, breakfast items together, baking items together. In a closet: hang by type (all shirts, all pants), then by color within each type. The system should be obvious enough that they maintain it themselves after you leave.
  3. 3Never buy bins before the sort. A common beginner mistake is buying organizing bins before knowing what's going in them. Always do the full sort first, measure the space, then recommend specific bins. A standard pantry needs about 6–8 matching bins. You can order them on Amazon for the client and add the cost to the invoice.
  4. 4Watch one pantry organizing video. Search YouTube: "how to organize a pantry from scratch." You'll see the full process — empty it, sort it, categorize it, put it back in zones. One video is enough to understand the whole job. The physical execution is straightforward once you've seen it done.

You don't need a professional camera — a modern phone shoots better than most DSLRs did 10 years ago. What you actually need to learn is light, editing, and how to pose people.

What this job is: You photograph people at an outdoor location for 1–2 hours, edit the best shots on your phone, and deliver them within 48 hours. You charge per session. Most clients are seniors, families, and graduations — they need photos they can actually post and print.
  1. 1Learn the golden hour rule. Shoot within 1–2 hours of sunrise or sunset only. Midday sun creates harsh shadows under the eyes and makes everyone squint. Golden hour light is soft, warm, and flattering on every face. Schedule all sessions in this window. It's the single biggest quality upgrade and costs you nothing.
  2. 2Learn Lightroom Mobile basics. Download Lightroom Mobile (free). Search YouTube: "Lightroom Mobile portrait editing for beginners." The core edits are: lower the highlights, raise the shadows, adjust the color temperature (warmer usually looks better), and sharpen slightly. These 4 adjustments fix 80% of photos. Save your settings as a preset so every session has a consistent look.
  3. 3Learn to direct people into natural poses. Most people freeze in front of a camera. "Stand there and smile" gives you a school photo. Instead: give them something to do — "walk toward me slowly," "look over your shoulder," "lean against that tree." Movement creates natural expressions. For families: have the kids run to the parents. Motion fixes awkwardness.
  4. 4Learn what makes a good location. You want: soft even light (shade or golden hour), a clean non-distracting background (trees, brick walls, open fields), and enough space to shoot wide. Avoid parking lots, cluttered streets, or anywhere with harsh direct sun. Find 3–4 reliable spots in your area and use them for every session.
Before your first paying client: Do 2–3 free shoots for friends or family at a golden hour location. For each shoot: practice directing them into poses, edit 10 photos in Lightroom, and deliver via a Google Photos shared album link. Do this until the whole process — shoot, edit, deliver — feels smooth and under 3 hours total.
